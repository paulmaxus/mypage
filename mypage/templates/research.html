Cochlear implants, listening effort and subtitles

I currently study how humans adapt to degraded signals such as noise-vocoded speech using behavioural tests and measuring pupil size. Vocoders simulate the experience that users of a cochlear implant have when listening to speech. My research goal is to determine the optimal amount of subtitles needed to enhance speech recognition while lowering listening effort. An application could be machine-generated words displayed on a modified watch or glasses.

Cochlear implants (CIs) convey only little spectral information which makes it hard to track fluctuations in pitch. Hence, the perception of music is diminished. Below, you can listen to examples from different music genres and speech, and switch between the natural sound and the CI simulation. You may notice that rhythmic music (e.g., Hip Hop) is much easier to listen to than classical music. This is because the temporal information is preserved in CIs.

In simple terms, we are exploring a method that can tell us where a person is looking without actually moving her eyes! Specifically, we study two brain wave components that can be obtained from electrophysiological data (i.e., electrodes attached to the scalp). The electrodes are placed symmetrically on both sides of the parieto-occipital scalp so that activation patterns of each hemisphere can be compared. If the activation is stronger on one side, it is more likely that the opposite side is attended to (decussation). Our first experiment is documented in my thesis defense.
