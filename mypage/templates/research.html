at UCL: adapting to degraded speech

I currently study how humans adapt to degraded signals such as noise-vocoded speech. This distortion simulates the experience that users of a cochlear implant have when listening to speech.

Listen to the example below. Is it hard to understand? Check out the solution, then listen to it again. If it appears clearer now then this is what we call a top-down effect. Your brain knows what to look for in the signal!


How humans learn and adapt is an exciting research question. It is believed that learning follows a so called power-law function. When you start practicing, you will learn a lot in a short amount of time. But the better you get, the slower the progress.

at Cimec: covert visuo-spatial attention

In simple terms, we were exploring a method that can tell you where a person is looking without actually moving her eyes!

Specifically, we studied two brain wave components that can be obtained from electrophysiological data (i.e., electrodes attached to the scalp). The electrodes we were interested in are placed symmetrically on both sides of the scalp so that activation patterns of each hemisphere can be compared. If the activation is stronger on one side, it is more likely that the opposite side is attended to (decussation).
